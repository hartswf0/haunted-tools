MAKITA — RETRO PRECISION

Makita Torque‑Regulator™ Ethics Driver

Torque‑ethics mistakes motion for meaning. The Ethics Driver offers twelve tidy clutch settings labeled with virtues, yet none of them engage the joists that hold a house up: budget, veto power, data rights, ownership. A clutch limits force; it does not create structure. We tuned ours from “Well‑Being 1” to “Justice 12” and watched screws polish the surface of simulated beams while the load path remained unchanged.

Procedures love adjustability because adjustability feels like agency. In practice, the tool’s feedback loop is between the operator and the tool itself—loud whirring, little shear strength. The workpiece (people, contracts, institutions) hears a performance, not a fastener biting into wood.

Doing ≠ Due. Force applied to a checklist does not transfer rights, resources, or risk.

Field test. We ran a “compliance frame retrofit” on a mock org chart. The driver excelled at aligning panels labeled “values,” “principles,” and “toolkit.” When asked to sink lag bolts into the line items called discretionary budget and decision rights, the bit free‑spun. No pilot holes were provided; the veneer delaminated.

What changes hands? If an ethics process leaves control untouched—no money moved, no IP shared, no governance seats ceded—it has not fastened anything. It has orbited the surface. The torque readout is impressive; the beam remains floating.

Warranty language (annotated): “Use of this tool implies a commitment to continuous improvement.” [Note: commitment is rhetorical, not contractual.]

Power Transfer Meter

Talk → Transfer: 0%

Bill of Materials — Metaphor

Head: Shiny virtue labels that slip under load. Shaft: Facilitation hours. Handle: Moral urgency grip. Battery: Institutional patience (finite). None of these are shear‑rated.

Failure modes. Cam‑out: language spins, credibility strips. Over‑drill: participants fatigue, disengage. Under‑pilot: no path into policy wood. Remediation requires a drill press called charter and a vice named binding authority.

Power shows up as transfers: budget, veto, seats, data, time. If none moved, nothing moved.

Recommendation. Pair the Ethics Driver with a structural kit: publish decision rights, carve out a budget line, assign a stop‑work clause. Then—only then—choose a clutch.

RYOBI — RETRO DIY

Ryobi Pastel‑Green Access Drill Kit™

Access is not ownership; a charger is not a grid. The kit promises participation—“everybody drills”—yet omits the one part that ties aspiration to sustained use: the power supply. The politics are in the outlet map, not the colorway of the handle.

We trialed the kit in a community shop. Sign‑ups blossomed, selfies multiplied, and the waiting list lengthened into a metric for “engagement.” Then the batteries died. Without shared charging infrastructure—budgeted electricity, staffed check‑in, replace‑on‑failure policy—the program converted enthusiasm into a queue. Inclusion became staging, not capacity.

If you can use it only while the gatekeeper watches, you don’t have a tool; you have a tour.

Combustion risk. Redistribution triggers heat. The kit’s plastic warms most when someone asks where the outlets are and who controls them. Safety data sheets warn of thermal events during “unauthorized load sharing.” In plain terms: plug more people in, trip more breakers owned by the same few.

Design note. Beige backdrops and rounded typography suggest approachability. But nothing in the palette routes funds toward public charging, or rewrites policies that forbid sharing sockets across departments. The friendly tone masks a hard boundary: no conduit provided.

Outlet Map

Status: Unpowered

How to Mean Access

Inventory the outlets. Publish a map. Who holds keys? What hours? What peak loads? Budget the electrons. If electricity isn’t prepaid for people without cards, “access” is an RSVP to disappointment.

Swap ownership, not just optics. Place the charger in the commons. Assign replacement authority to the users, not the sponsor. Write the right to recharge into policy, not a press release.

Warranty (annotated): “Kit promotes inclusion.” [Include a charger & policy amendment; otherwise, it promotes waiting.]

DEWALT — RETRO INDUSTRIAL

DeWalt Citation Hammer™ (Yellow/Black Edition)

Citations authorize the blow. Swing the hammer and watch objections flatten under the mass of precedent. But a joist cares little for authority; it cares for fastener length, angle, and the load path to ground. The Citation Hammer drives doctrine, not anchors.

We tested it in a governance meeting. “Best practice” nails landed with satisfying thunks. The wall seemed solid until a lateral load (an unanticipated stakeholder) pushed; the panel sheared at the thin places where evidence had been stapled, not bolted.

“Because the literature says so” is a mallet blow that quiets a room and leaves the structure unchanged.

Stencil aesthetics. Hazard yellow and black imply seriousness—and risk. Yet the danger is mislocated: the hazard is not the worker who questions the plan; it’s the plan that confuses conformity with integrity.

Use it where it belongs. Standards are shop drawings, not the building. They help when paired with calculations, tolerances, and—especially—remedies if the part fails. Without that, you have signage, not safety.

Remedies Ladder

Status: Signage only

Remedies Over References

Before swinging: Publish the remedy path. Who can halt? Who pays for rework? What compensates those harmed? Put these in reach like emergency stops.

After impact: If the only recourse is “file a comment,” you’ve installed a suggestion box where a brace should be. Install the brace.

Warranty (annotated): “Void if questioned.” [Translate: designed to be brandished, not tested.]

MILWAUKEE — RETRO POWER

Milwaukee Messiness Fuel™ Grinder Kit

“Embrace the messiness” is a slogan that sells sparks. Real grinders remove material; managerial grinders produce spectacle. This kit arrives pre‑guarded, pre‑approved, and pre‑narrated. The sparks stay inside the tray; the edges never change.

We staged a “chaos sprint.” Safety rails ensured no scope could escape its lane. Out‑of‑band ideas were redirected to a parking lot that resembled a slag heap—glittering, inert. At the end, a photo collage of “innovation” was mailed to stakeholders. Nothing sharp was produced.

If the risk is fully theatrical, the creativity is fully symbolic.

Hot‑rod veneer. Lightning bolts and checkers imply speed. But every bolt lands on a bumper that says “not here, not now, not funded.” The chrome reflects the shop lights while hiding the absence of a motor.

When to use. Use spectacle consciously—as ritual closure after the real cutting is funded, not as a substitute for cutting. Celebrate edges you actually changed.

Spectacle → Cut Depth

Cut depth: 10% · Mode: Stagecraft

Cut Path Checklist

Material identified? What exactly will be removed? Authority secured? Who signs the sparks waiver? Budgeted? New tooling and PPE assigned? Without answers, cancel the stunt.

Warranty (annotated): Includes guard rails. [Keeps you safe from change.]

SPECIAL — POWER SOURCE

Transformative Justice FLEXVOLT™ Battery

Capacity without circuits is hope in a brick. The battery promises 60V forever, which is to say: infinite potential, zero path. World‑building requires wiring diagrams—institutions, duties, escrow accounts, mutual aid—through which energy flows predictably under load.

We placed the brick on three projects. In each, its gravitational field drew cameras, grants, and adjectives. But when the switch flipped—when rent relief needed funds, when parole support needed hours, when archives needed stewards—the brick stayed full, heavy, and ornamental.

Utopia that cannot run a circuit is décor.

Why the metaphor persists. Batteries are portable; systems are not. It is easier to pose with capacity than to maintain a line. But a community runs on lines: dues, rotations, ledgers, repair.

Wiring diagram placeholder

Wire the Future

Conduit: legal agreements that move authority. Breakers: stop‑work clauses for harm. Loads: named obligations with time and money attached. Ground: public, auditable ledgers.

Catalog note (annotated): “World‑building power.” [Add diagrams, or stop claiming power.]

ACADEMIA — PERFORMATIVE SERIES

Cordless Good Academic Drill™ (Virtue Edition)

Citations recharge reputation. The drill spins faster with each reference, and the workshop applauds as footnotes glow. Yet reputational torque strips screws of credibility when applied to materials called lived stakes and timelines. The bit needs friction with accountability, not applause.

We assigned it to a syllabus renovation. The deck dazzled; the readings multiplied. The adjunct pay line stayed flat. The drill performed beautifully on the veneer while joists groaned under unpaid labor.

If your ethics lives in the bibliography alone, it lives nowhere.

Best use. Great for pilot holes: making space for others’ work, citing to pay, pointing to co‑authorship with material transfer. Poor for final tightening: that requires hiring lines, shared governance, and redistribution of credit.

Syllabus wireframe

Accountability Bits

Bit A: Budgeted citation—allocate cash with the reading list. Bit B: Co‑teaching credit written into policy. Bit C: Time release for staff who carry the course’s hidden labor.

Warning (annotated): Overuse strips credibility. [Apply with material follow‑through.]

LEDGER — PUNCH LIST

Infinite Due Punch List™ (Contractor’s Nightmare)

Some jobsites are designed never to close. A deficit model keeps carpenters on ladders while the building owner collects rent on “ongoing improvements.” Ethics discourse often inherits this model: “work remains,” “ever better,” “continuous refinement.” The bill is eternal; the debtor is always the worker.

We audited a year of punch‑list items. Many were rephrasings of the same problem: structural harms routed to surface fixes. The list created the feeling of progress while preventing the act of completion that would trigger hand‑over and warranty liability.

Completion is the most radical step in a system addicted to perpetual repair.

Closeout protocol. Define the due in measurable terms: transfers executed, rights recorded, harms compensated. Publish a date when the site signs off and the owner—whoever benefits—takes responsibility.

Closeout checklist mock

From Doing to Due

Doing: meetings, workshops, toolkits. Due: money, time, land, data, seats. If your ledger lists the former without the latter, you are paying in noise.

Slogan (annotated): “Some jobsites haunt you forever.” [Only if we profit from the haunting.]

MYTHOLOGIES — METHOD

🛠 Haunted Tools as Mythologies

Method: The Semiotic Scalpel. Following Roland Barthes' Mythologies, we begin with the form (the object as seen: colors, labels, materials), expose the concept (the ideological function that object serves), then deliver the twist (the cut that reveals how the sign performs as myth).

The aim is not exhaustiveness but semiotic incision—brief, witty cuts that reveal how everyday objects carry ideological freight. Each “tool” becomes a miniature essay in the Barthesian tradition: part cultural criticism, part comedy, all aimed at denaturalizing what passes as natural.

MYTHOLOGIES — 1

Makita Torque‑Regulator™ Ethics Driver

Myth: The ethics toolkit—clutch with twelve settings: “Justice 12,” “Well‑Being 1.”

Form: Precise, adjustable, turquoise housing, numbered dial—medical‑grade vibes.

Concept: Ethics as calibration rather than structure; injustice as mis‑setting, not power.

In action: Frameworks add dials and modules; virtue multiplies as transfers vanish.

Semiotic function: The adjustability sign substitutes for the transfer sign.

Punchline: Spins endlessly without resistance—karaoke for the morally ambitious.
